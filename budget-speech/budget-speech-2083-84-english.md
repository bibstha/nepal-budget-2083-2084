Honorable Speaker of the House of Representatives,
Honorable Chairperson of the National Assembly,

**1.** It is with great pride that I rise in this honorable House to continue the tradition — initiated about 75 years ago (on 21 Magh 2008 B.S.) by Shri Suvarna Shamsher, the first Finance Minister of modern Nepal — of presenting the country's statement of revenue and expenditure. The objective of that time, "a better standard of living for the people," remains just as relevant today. On this occasion I pay heartfelt tribute to the martyrs who sacrificed their lives in the various movements for civil liberty, democratic rights, social justice and a prosperous Nepal, and I express my solidarity with the citizens injured in those movements.

**2.** The awakening of a change-seeking youth force that rose in favor of accountable governance, integrity and civic dignity, and the vast popular mandate obtained on the new political ground thus established, have entrusted the present government with extraordinary responsibility. As Finance Minister, I have accepted this not merely as a constitutional formality but as a duty to transform the character of the state, the culture of governance and the very form of the economy. I am confident that this budget will become a credible policy instrument that meets the legitimate expectations of all Nepalis at home and abroad for a production-oriented economy, international prestige and technology-friendly change.

**3.** With the generational transition in Nepal's politics, there is an ardent anticipation of transformation across every dimension of national life. Embracing the favorable conditions brought by irreversible change, we are committed to establishing standards of good governance through refinement in policy, law, institutions, systems, processes, leadership and intent. Ending policy drift, procrastination, institutional capture and the plunder of state resources — and building a results-oriented system of governance — is the need of the hour. Although the present constitution has laid an important foundation for breaking the cycle of social and cultural exclusion, deeper structural reform is needed for equitable access to economic opportunities.

**4.** The time has come to grapple with the challenges and opportunities created by the specialized diversification and limitless expansion of information and communication and artificial intelligence, of finance, trade and investment; by the collective strength of the Nepali community spread across the world; and by the aggressive adoption of technology. The responsibility of building a virtuous cycle of equitable economic growth rests on our shoulders. On this journey, on behalf of the government, I make a heartfelt request to the Federal Parliament, all organs of the state, civil society and all the Nepali people for constructive suggestions and cooperation.

**5.** The indicators of the country's current economic condition, the obstacles to progress, and possible paths to their solution have been made public in the "Current Economic Situation Paper, 2083." The principles and priorities of next fiscal year's policies and programs and of the Appropriation Bill have already shed light on the government's substantive policy direction. Likewise, the detailed statistical account — the "Economic Survey, 2082/83" — has already been tabled in Parliament.

**6.** The country now stands at a decisive juncture of sweeping economic reform. The vicious cycle of political instability has ended. Most macroeconomic indicators are improving. The financial system has ample liquidity along with robust foreign-exchange reserves; there is vast potential in clean-energy production and new technology; and the youth demographic stands as a great form of capital. Nevertheless, instability in international supply chains, sluggishness in the domestic market, low investment in the productive sector, and distrust toward public institutions remain as challenges. The aim of this budget is to transform these challenges into opportunities and to simultaneously raise the state's service-delivery capacity, the private sector's confidence, and citizens' trust.

Honorable Speaker, Honorable Chairperson — at the outset, I announce some notable reforms related to the foundation of the all-round development that this FY 2083/84 budget seeks to establish.

**7.** To provide relief to enterprises and businesses, expand the middle class, and energize the overall economy, I have undertaken a broad review of tax rates.

**(a)** To reduce the tax burden, I have doubled the income-tax exemption threshold, raising it to NPR 1 million (ten lakh) for individuals. I have reduced the maximum rate of personal income tax by ten percentage points.

I have reduced customs duty on 273 types of raw materials so that the duty on industrial raw materials is at least one tier below that on finished goods. I have limited the existing eleven-tier customs-duty structure to seven tiers.

I have abolished the excise duty currently levied on 360 goods. I have consolidated scattered taxes collected at the customs point — such as the infrastructure-development tax and the road repair and improvement charge — into the green tax.

I have provided that Section 57 of the Income Tax Act, 2058, shall not apply to involuntary disposal upon the death of a natural person, nor to a change of ownership in a controlled entity arising from such a change of ownership. *(This clause is partly garbled in the source OCR.)*

I have arranged that the capital gains tax levied on the sale of listed companies' securities shall be final.

We will implement a provision under which consumers making purchases through digital payment receive a 10 percent discount on value added tax at the moment the invoice is issued. We will automate the VAT refund system. To promote the culture of issuing and taking invoices in the purchase and sale of goods and services, attractive programs such as a lottery will be launched.

We will form a high-level committee to study and submit recommendations on the relevance of multiple VAT rates.

Adopting a policy of making tax compliance a partner in development rather than a punishment, I have introduced a special scheme on tax exemption and relief — to resolve long-standing tax disputes created by legal ambiguity and taxpayer ignorance, and to create a business-friendly environment.

To resolve tax disputes pending before courts or judicial bodies — whether brought by the government or the taxpayer — I have arranged that if the taxpayer pays the assessed tax amount plus one percent within the prescribed deadline and withdraws the case, the fees, fines, additional charges, late fees, and interest shall be waived.

**8.** The Government of Nepal's existing cumbersome organizational structure will now be made lean. Given that the number of federal ministries has already been fixed at 18 (down from 22), I have adopted a policy of using the funds saved by cutting departments, offices, and positions to make the administration efficient. We will abolish 31 bodies, merge 6, transfer 6, and restructure 18. With the aim of making the bureaucracy capable of a dignified livelihood, high-performing, efficient, and accountable, arrangements have been made for the continuous and respectable increase of the service benefits of civil, military, police, and teacher personnel.

**9.** Cost reduction and efficiency enhancement will be another dimension of institutional reform. To this end, we will control recurrent expenditure while maintaining unprecedented frugality and allocative efficiency. I have substantially reduced office operating costs. I have removed allocations for training expenses outside training centers and for equipment such as cameras and televisions. We will end the practice of creating various funds to provide perks. I have removed the discounts given to officials and staff of public enterprises on the purchase of goods and services produced by those enterprises. Until the adjustment of positions and management of liabilities of merged and abolished bodies is completed, I have frozen the amounts allocated to those bodies. These steps are preliminarily estimated to save about NPR 20 billion.

**10.** Appropriate policy and legal arrangements will be made for investment promotion, economic reform, and smooth service delivery. For this, we will draft, replace, or amend dozens of acts, rules, procedures, and directives. To immediately repeal the 15 laws already announced, we will present a Bill to Amend Certain Nepal Acts in Parliament.

**(a)** By amending company law, we will bring clarity on matters such as conflict of interest and disclosure, and ease the company-liquidation process. We will conclude foreign-investment protection and double-taxation-avoidance agreements with additional countries. By amending the Insolvency Act, 2063, we will help resolve the financial problems of consumers and of micro, small, and medium enterprises. To promote investment, we will draft a limited liability partnership law, which will encourage angel investment to flow into venture capital and private equity funds.

We will ease the arrangements allowing Nepali citizens to invest abroad. By amending the Industrial Enterprises Act, we will make it sufficient merely to notify the Department of Industry on matters such as capacity expansion, change of ownership, and capital increase. By amending the Foreign Investment and Technology Transfer Act, we will provide that prior approval of Nepal Rastra Bank is not required for the repatriation of investment and that mere notification suffices. We will bring convertible instruments, project-tied funding, and other hybrid instruments within the scope of foreign investment. We will remove the prior-approval requirement within the automatic-approval process for foreign investment. We will simplify the process of remitting service fees, royalties, and technology payments abroad. We will make legal provisions for the recovery of bad debts, and will soon draft a law on intellectual-property protection. We will arrange that credit can be extended based on a creditworthiness assessment of individuals and businesses derived from analysis of their past economic activity. We will establish a separate tribunal for the swift resolution of commercial disputes, and reform the Mediation Act.

To enable non-resident Nepalis to participate in the secondary securities market, we will amend the legal provisions on foreign-investment approval, accounting of investment, profit repatriation, and capital gains tax.

By amending the National Civil Code, 2074, we will provide that foreign investors in Nepal, international organizations, and branches of multinational companies may take on long-term lease — for residential purposes — buildings in the residential sector (not exceeding 25 percent of total units) or apartments at locations specified by the government.

**(f)** To ensure financial access for small and medium entrepreneurs, we will guarantee loans on a "first-loss recovery" basis. *(Partly garbled in the source OCR.)*

**11.** Committing to launch an aggressive series of operational reforms and to change the pace and rhythm of capital expenditure, we will work in "mission mode" — improving the procurement process, ensuring flexibility in resource management (including reallocation), mobilizing alternative finance, and ensuring the stability of project chiefs — to complete projects at the prescribed cost and on time. A sunset law on development projects will be presented in Parliament within the current fiscal year. We will arrange tracking so that mobilization-advance funds can be spent only on the relevant project. A preliminary pipeline of infrastructure projects that can be built under the hybrid-annuity model will be ready within three months. We will suitably address the difficulties faced by construction businesses due to the rise in the price of fuel, bitumen, and other construction materials caused by the Middle East conflict.
